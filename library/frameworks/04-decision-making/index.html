Decision-Making Frameworks

Five frameworks I use when someone arrives stuck. Most people are not struggling because they lack intelligence. They are struggling because they lack clarity.

Over the years, I have realized that most people are not struggling because they lack intelligence. They are struggling because they lack clarity.

Whenever someone approaches me with a difficult decision, I usually guide them through one or more of the following frameworks. None of them is original. All of them are useful. The right one depends on what kind of stuckness you are in.

Framework 1 — Purpose Before Path

The first question I ask is: “Why do you want this?”

Most people are trying to optimise a path without understanding the destination. Before evaluating options, clarify:

  • What outcome are you seeking?
  • What problem are you trying to solve?
  • What would success actually look like?

A clear purpose eliminates many wrong choices automatically. Whenever I feel stuck, I try to move one layer deeper into understanding the real reason behind the decision. If you can name the destination cleanly, half the path-finding is already done.

Framework 2 — The Future Self Test

Imagine yourself five years from today. You are looking back at this decision.

  • Which choice would make your future self proud?
  • Which choice aligns with the person you want to become?

Short-term comfort and long-term growth often point in different directions. I generally trust the option that aligns with long-term character development rather than short-term convenience.

Framework 3 — Advice to a Friend

This is perhaps the most powerful framework I know.

When we make decisions for ourselves, emotions, fears and insecurities distort our thinking. When we advise a friend, clarity appears.

I ask: “What advice would you give someone you deeply care about if they were facing this exact situation?”

The answer is often already known. The friend-frame removes ego, defensiveness, and the small lies we tell ourselves to keep an option alive that should have been closed weeks ago.

Framework 4 — Reversible vs Irreversible

Many people treat reversible decisions as if they are permanent.

Most career decisions, learning decisions and professional opportunities can be adjusted later. Very few decisions are truly irreversible. The goal is not perfect certainty. The goal is making the best decision possible with the information available today — and being honest with yourself about whether you can change course later if you need to.

If a decision is reversible, speed matters more than perfection. If a decision is genuinely irreversible, care matters more than speed. Sorting into the right bucket is half the work.

Framework 5 — The Freedom Test

I often ask: “Which option creates greater long-term freedom?”

Freedom does not always mean ease. Sometimes the difficult path creates more future freedom than the comfortable path. I generally prefer choices that increase optionality, learning and long-term resilience — even when the comfortable path is easier in the short run.

My philosophy is simple:

Clarity creates better choices. Better choices create freedom. Freedom creates the ability to serve.
