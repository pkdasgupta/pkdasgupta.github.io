Clarity Over Certainty

We confuse the two all the time. Certainty pretends to know what comes next; clarity simply sees what is true now. The first kills movement; the second creates it.

A friend wrote to me last winter, mid-decision. Should I take the new role? She had been running the numbers for weeks: comp delta, equity vesting, scope, title, commute, the team’s reputation, her current manager’s politics. She had spreadsheets. She had a colour-coded weighted-decision matrix. She had insomnia.

What she did not have was an answer.

I read the email twice and wrote back: “You are looking for certainty. You will not find it. What you can build is clarity. Tell me, in one sentence, what you are actually optimising for.”

She sent two paragraphs back about title and TC.

I asked again. In one sentence.

The third email was just five words.

“I want to feel respected.”

Within an hour she had decided. The new role was not a place where she would feel respected, and that mattered more than the title bump that had been keeping her awake.

The two are not the same thing

Certainty is the wish that the future would behave. It is predictive. It is the itch we scratch by gathering more data, polling more people, running another projection. The promise of certainty is that we will only have to make this decision once.

Clarity is the smaller, harder thing. It is descriptive. It just asks: what is actually going on? What is the situation, in plain words? What do I actually want? What am I willing to give up?

You can be clear and uncertain at the same time. In fact, that is the most honest place a thinking person can be. I do not know what will happen, but I know what I want, and I know which moves I will not regret.

You cannot be certain and clear at the same time, because certainty requires you to lie to yourself about the things you cannot know.

Why we reach for certainty anyway

Certainty is comforting. It promises that if we just gather enough data, talk to enough people, run the model one more time, we will arrive at the right answer, and the right answer will protect us from the embarrassment of being wrong.

This is a fantasy. The right answer almost never reveals itself in advance — not for career moves, not for marriages, not for moves to a new city, not for choosing a school for your child. The reason is simple: most important life decisions involve another person, or a future version of you, neither of whom can be modelled in a spreadsheet.

What can be modelled is what you will regret if you do not at least try. That is a clarity question, not a certainty question.

How to actually find clarity

I have been working with mentees on this for years now, and I notice the same moves keep working:

One. Write the question down. Most decision paralysis dissolves the moment you write the actual question on paper. The version in your head is fuzzier than you think.

Two. Find the one-sentence version. Whatever you wrote, cut it in half. Then cut it in half again. The five-word version is closer to the truth than the five-paragraph version.

Three. Name the thing you are most afraid to say out loud. It is usually the answer.

Four. Write down what you would tell a younger sibling in your situation. You are wiser about other people than you are about yourself. Borrow your own wisdom.

Five. Sleep on it. Not metaphorically — actually sleep. Most clarity arrives, fully formed, in the first quiet hour of the next morning. The mind sorts overnight if you give it the chance.

A small confession

I do not always live by this. I am writing partly because I had a week last month where I was hunting for certainty about a work decision, and a colleague — with the perfect timing of someone who has read you all your life — sent me a one-line message:

“You’re trying to feel certain. Try to feel clear instead.”

It worked. It always works. That is why I keep coming back to it, and why I think it is the most useful sentence I know.

The point

Certainty is a trap because the world does not deal in it. Clarity is a gift because it costs nothing and changes everything.

If you can give one human being one moment of clarity in their life, you have done a good day’s work. If you can do it for yourself, you can do it for others.

That is the whole project.

— PKD
